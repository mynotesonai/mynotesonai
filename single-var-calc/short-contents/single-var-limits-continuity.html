Limits and Continuity

The central idea behind the concept of limit is approcahing some point without reaching that point. The reason we want to know limit of a function is because limits allow us to know the value of a function evenif the function is undefined.

The limit of a function f(x) is the y value the function f(x) approches at a given value of x, regardless of whether the funcion actually reaches that value.

Consider the function $ f(x) = x + 5 $. When we plug x = 5 into the function we get $ f(x) = 5 + 5 $. Hence 10 is the limit of the function. As the function approaches 5 as the value of x, the value of y gets closer and closer to 10.

The below function returns an indeterminant form $ \frac{0}{0} $ but if we see the graph of this function we can see that this function is undefined only when $ x = 3 $

The concept of limit helps us to evaluate the function of indeterminant form

Limit Notation

$$ \Large{\lim_{x \to 2} f(x) = 5} $$

General vs One sided limits

In certain scenarios we evaluate the limit when we approach the value from a particular side, that is the left or the right of the function. This is called a one-sided limit.

If f(x) does not approach a specific finite value as x approaches a from both the directions, then we can say that the limit does not exist. We can say that the general limit exists when both the left-hand and the right-hand side limits exist and the value of the left-hand side limit is equal to the right-hand side limit.

$$ \large{\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = 5 \ \ \text{if and only if} \ \ \lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x)} $$

Evaluating limits

Evaluating limits means pluggin in the value and simplify. $$ \large{\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)} \\ \large{\lim_{x \to 4} [x^{2} + 7x - 9] = (4)^{2} + 7(4) - 9 = 35} $$

However we can get an indeterminant form even after plugging in the value.

Fundamental theorems of limit
Sum Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) + \lim_{x \to a} g(x) $$
Difference Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - g(x) = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) - \lim_{x \to a} g(x) $$
Constant Multiple Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} cf(x) = c \ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) $$
Product Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} (f(x) . g(x)) = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) . \lim_{x \to a} g(x) $$
Product Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} (f(x) \ . \ g(x)) = \lim_{x \to a} f(x) \ . \ \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \ \ \text{if} \ \ \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \neq 0 $$
Power Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} (f(x))^{n}) = \frac{\lim_{x \to a} f(x)}{\lim_{x \to a} g(x)} \ \ \text{if} \ \ \lim_{x \to a} g(x) \neq 0 $$
Root Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} \sqrt[n]{f(x)} = \sqrt[n]{\lim_{x \to a} f(x)} $$
Constant Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} c = c $$
Direct Substitution Law $$ \lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a) $$
