Length, Dot Product, Cross Product

In a 2D space the distance between $P_1(x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2(x_2, y_2)$ is $$ d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^{2} + (y_2 - y_1)^{2}} $$ This is the same as the length of the vector $\vec(v) = \vec{P_1P_2} $

The Norm of the Vector

$$ \vec{|| v ||} = \sqrt{v_{1}^{2} + v_{2}^{2} + v_{3}^{2}} \ ( \ \text{or} \ \ || v || = \sqrt{v_{1}^{2} + v_{2}^{2} + v_{3}^{2} + ...... + v_{2}^{n}} \ ) $$ the zero vector $\vec{0}$ has length 0.

The Unit Vector

The vector of length 1 is called a unit vector. If $ \vec{v} \neq \vec{0} $, then $\frac{1}{\vec{|| v ||}} \times \vec{v}$ is a unit vector. There are a few unit vectors which are important. $$ \vec{i} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \ \text{,} \ \vec{i} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \ \ \text{in 2D space} $$ $$ \vec{i} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \ \ \vec{j} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \ \ \vec{k} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \ \ \text{in 3D space} $$ $$ \text{This is because for any vector} \vec{v} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ v_2 \end{bmatrix} \\ \ \ = v_1 \times \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} + v_2 \times \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} $$

Dot Product

A Dot product is a vector operation that tells us how much two vectors a and b go in the same direction. If the result of dot product is 0 then we can conclude that these two vectors are perpendicular or have $90^{\circ}$ $$ \text{Let} \ \ \vec{u} . \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 + v_1 \\ u_2 + v_2 \end{bmatrix} $$ The length of a vector $ \vec{|| v ||} $ can be expressed as follows $$ \large{\vec{|| v ||} = \sqrt{\vec{v} . \vec{v}}} $$

Relation between dot product and the Euclidean Geometric notion of Angle

Let $ u_1 \ text{and} \ v_1 $ be non-zero vectors in 2D-space or 3D-space, and let $\theta$ be the angle between $\vec{u} \ \text{and} \ \vec{v} $. Then $$ \vec{|| v ||} = \vec{|| v ||}\vec{|| v ||}\cos \theta $$ $$ \cos \theta = \frac{\vec{u} . \vec{v}}{\vec{|| u ||}\vec{|| v ||}} $$ $$ \vec{| u |} . \vec{| v |} <= \vec{|| u ||}\vec{|| v ||} $$

Orthogonal Projections

Let $ vec{u} \ text{and} \ vec{b} $ two vectors in 2-space or 3-space. Then the vector $vec{w_1}$ forms an orthogonal projection of $vec{u} \text{on} vec{b} $, and is denoted by $proj_\vec{b} \vec{u} = k\vec{b}$

Cross Product

If $\vec{u}\begin{bmatrix} u_1 \\ u_2 \\ u_3 \end{bmatrix} and \ \vec{v}\begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix} $ are two vectors, then the cross product between the two $\vec{u} \times \vec{v}$ is a vector given by $$ \vec{u} \times \vec{v} = \begin{bmatrix} u_2 & u_3 \\ v_2 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} \ \vec{i} - \begin{bmatrix} u_1 & u_3 \\ v_1 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} \ \vec{j} + \begin{bmatrix} u_1 & u_2 \\ v_1 & v_2 \end{bmatrix} \ \vec{k} $$ $$ \begin{bmatrix} \vec{i} & \vec{j} & \vec{k} \\ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \\ v_2 & v_2 & v_3 \end{bmatrix} $$

Properties of Cross Product

$ \vec{u} \ \times \ \vec{v} \ \text{is orthogonal to both} \ \vec{u} \ \text{and} \ \vec{v} \ \ \text{i.e.} \ \ \vec{u} . (\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) = 0 \ \ \text{and} \ \ \vec{v} . (\vec{u} \times \vec{v}) = 0 $ $ || \vec{u} \times \vec{v} || = || \vec{u} || . || \vec{v} \ \sin\theta $
