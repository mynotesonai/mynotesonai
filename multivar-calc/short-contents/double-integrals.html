Double Integrals

Double integrals allow us to find the volume V under a multivariable function f(x, y) over the $\mathbf{R} = [a, b] \times [c, d]$ Using double integrals we can approximate the volume over the region R. Following is an example of approximating the volume over the region R using upper right corners

Midpoint rule for double integrals

The volume under a multivariable function f(x,y) over a Region $ R = [a, b] \times [c, d] $ can be approximated by using the following double Riemann sum with midpoints. $$ \displaystyle \int \displaystyle \int f(x, y) \ dA \approx \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{i=1}^n f(\overline{x_i}, \overline{y_i}) \nabla A $$

m is the number of units in the x-direction and n is the number of units in the y-direction in the region R. The larger the volume m and n, the more accurate the approximation will be.

Example of the Midpoint rule for double integrals

Approximatiog the following double integral using the midpoint rule. $$ \displaystyle \int \displaystyle \int xe^{xy}dA \ m=n=2 \ R=[0,2] \times x[0,1] $$ $$ \text{The formula of the midpoint rule} \displaystyle \int \displaystyle \int f(x,y) dA \approx \sum_{i=1}^m \sum_{i=1}^n f(\overline{x_i}, \overline{y_i}) \nabla A $$ In this scenario I have to use the midpoint rule to approximate the volume. The expression $R=[0,2] \times x[0,1]$ tells us the area that we have to apprximate and $m=n=2$ says how many units can smaller rectangles can we have in the x-axis and how many in the y-axis.
