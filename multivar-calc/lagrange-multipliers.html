Lagrange Multipliers

In many optimization problems, we need to find necessary conditions for a function $J : \Omega \rightarrow \mathbf{R}$ to have a local extremum with respect to U of $ \Omega $ (where $ \Omega $ is open). A lagrange multiplier is a method that is used in the method of optimization, for minimizing and maximizing a function subject to some constraint on the variables. In single variable scenario

Lagrange Multiplier Example

$$ \text{$f(x, y, z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$ if $x + y + z = 1$, and $x + 2y + 3z = 6$}$$ $$ g(x, y, z) = x + y + z - 1 $$ $$ h(x, y, z) = x + 2y + 3z - 6 $$

Solution
$$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x $$ $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2y $$ $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} = 2z $$
$$ \frac{\partial g}{\partial x} = 1 $$ $$ \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} = 1 $$ $$ \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} = 2z $$
$$ \frac{\partial h}{\partial x} = 1 $$ $$ \frac{\partial h}{\partial y} = 2 $$ $$ \frac{\partial h}{\partial z} = 3 $$

Formulating the lagrange multiplier equations and solve for x, y and z

$$ 2x = \lambda + \mu $$ $$ x = \frac{\lambda + \mu} {2} $$            
$$ 2x = \lambda + \mu $$ $$ y = \frac{\lambda + 2\mu} {2} $$            
$$ 2x = \lambda + \mu $$ $$ z = \frac{\lambda + 3\mu} {2} $$            

Plugging in the value of x, y and z into the constraint equation

$$ x = \frac{\lambda + \mu} {2} + y = \frac{\lambda + 2\mu} {2} + z = \frac{\lambda + 3\mu} {2} = 1$$

Multiplying both sides by 2

$$ \lambda + \mu + \lambda + 2\mu + \lambda + 3\mu = 2 $$

$$ 3\lambda + 6\mu = 2 \text{ ____ } (i) $$

$$ \frac{\lambda + \mu} {2} + 2(\frac{\lambda + 2\mu} {2}) + 2(\frac{\lambda + 3\mu} {2}) = 6$$ $$ \frac{\lambda + \mu} {2} + \frac{2\lambda + 4\mu} {2} + \frac{3\lambda + 9\mu} {2} = 6 $$ $$ 6\lambda + 14\mu = 12 $$ $$ 3\lambda + 7\mu = 6 \text{ ____ } (ii)$$

We will now use equations (i) and (ii) to solve for $\lambda$ and $\mu$
$$ 3\lambda + 6\mu = 2 $$ $$ (3\lambda + 7\mu = 6 ) $$ $$ \text{__________________________________} $$ $$ -\mu = -4 $$ Substituting $\mu$ in equation (i) we get
$$ 3\lambda + 6(4) = 2 $ $$ $$ 3\lambda + 24 = 2 $ $$ $$ \lambda = -\frac{22}{3} $$

Now that we have $\lambda$ and $\mu$ we can plug those values into the equations of x, y and z $$ \mu = 4 = \frac{12}{3} $$ $$ \lambda = -\frac{22}{3} $$ $$ x = \frac{-\frac{22}{3} + \frac{12}{3}}{2} $$ $$ x = -\frac{5}{3} $$ $$ y = \frac{-\frac{22}{3} + \frac{24}{3}}{2} $$ $$ y = \frac{1}{3} $$ $$ z = \frac{-\frac{22}{3} + \frac{36}{3}}{2} $$ $$ z = \frac{7}{3} $$ $$ x = -\frac{5}{3} \text{, } y = \frac{1}{3} , z = \frac{7}{3} $$

The last step is plug those values in the original function. these are the coordinate points and we are evaluating this function at that point to see what the value is $$ f(-\frac{5}{3}, \frac{1}{3}, \frac{7}{3}) = (-\frac{5}{3})^2 + (\frac{1}{3})^2 + (\frac{7}{3})^2 $$ $$ f(-\frac{5}{3}, \frac{1}{3}, \frac{7}{3}) = \frac{25}{3} $$

At this stage we know that $\frac{25}{3}$ is the extrema of the function evaluated at $ f(-\frac{5}{3}, \frac{1}{3}, \frac{7}{3}) = \frac{25}{3} $ Now we need to determine if this is the maxima or the minima. We know that this function $f(x, y, z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$ is a paraboloid that opens up.

So we know that there is maximum of the dunction because this shape increases forever in the upword direction. So we can conclude that the function with These two constraints have one minima at $f(-\frac{5}{3}, \frac{1}{3}, \frac{7}{3}) = \frac{25}{3}$ coordinates, that is $\frac{25}{3}$ and there are no maxima.
